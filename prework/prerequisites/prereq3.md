## Motivation
Math anxiety is real and common among people entering tech. This session normalizes that struggle and repositions math as a *thinking tool*, not a memorization challenge. In programming, you won't calculate square roots by hand—computers do that. But you *will* think logically about sequences, conditions, and relationships. That's what arithmetic, logic, and Boolean operators are training. More importantly, this session emphasizes **ownership**: showing your work, asking for help, and taking responsibility for filling gaps in your understanding. Ownership is what transforms an applicant into a professional. You own your learning. You own your mistakes. You own your growth.

## Objectives
By the end of this session, participants will:
- Understand that arithmetic is a foundation for logical thinking, not just calculation
- Recognize gaps in their math knowledge without shame or fear
- Practice showing work and explaining reasoning (critical for code reviews)
- Understand that asking for help and receiving feedback is a sign of ownership, not weakness
- Learn that Boolean logic (true/false) underlies all decision-making in code
- Develop confidence solving logic puzzles collaboratively
- See ownership as a core professional skill that applies to learning, feedback, and growth

## Specific Things to Learn

**Technical Skills:**
- Order of operations (PEMDAS: Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication/Division, Addition/Subtraction)
- Working with fractions (addition, subtraction, simplification)
- Algebraic vocabulary: variables, expressions, functions, formulas
- Boolean logic concepts: true, false, AND, OR, NOT
- Percentages and ratios
- Number bases: binary (base 2) and hexadecimal (base 16)
- Word problem solving: translating words into equations
- Logic puzzle problem-solving strategies

**Core "Soft" Skills:**
- Taking ownership of your learning and growth
- Showing work (transparency in thinking)
- Asking for help without shame
- Explaining your reasoning clearly
- Identifying and filling knowledge gaps
- Collaborative problem-solving
- Receiving feedback constructively

### Task 1: "Math Without a Calculator" Challenge (90 minutes)

**Objective:** Assess your current math skills, identify gaps, and practice showing your work.

**Instructions:**
Complete the following arithmetic and mathematical reasoning problems. **Show all your work by hand, using analog pen and paper.** Photograph each page of your completed work and and upload the images to a Google Doc.

**Order of Operations (PEMDAS)**
- 5 + 3 × 2 = ?
- (8 - 2) × 4 ÷ 2 = ?
- 2³ + 5 - 1 = ?
- 10 - 3 × 2 + 4 = ?

**Fractions**
- 1/2 + 1/3 = ?
- 3/4 - 1/8 = ?
- 2/3 × 3/5 = ?
- Simplify 8/12

**Percentages & Ratios**
- What is 15% of 80?
- If a ratio is 3:5 and the smaller value is 12, what's the larger value?
- A shirt costs $40. It's 20% off. What's the new price?

**Algebraic Thinking**
- If x = 5, what is 2x + 3?
- Solve: x + 7 = 15
- Write an expression for "five more than twice a number"
- If a function f(x) = 3x - 2, what is f(4)?

**Number Bases**
- Convert 13 (decimal) to binary
- Convert 1010 (binary) to decimal
- What is F in hexadecimal, converted to decimal? (Hint: A=10, B=11, C=12, D=13, E=14, F=15)

**Boolean Logic**
- True AND False = ?
- True OR False = ?
- NOT(True) = ?
- If x = 10, is the statement "x > 5 AND x < 15" true or false?

**Word Problems**
- Sarah has $50. She buys a book for $12 and a pen for $3. How much does she have left?
- A recipe calls for 2 cups of flour and serves 4 people. You're cooking for 6 people. How much flour do you need?
- A store is offering 25% off all items. If an item originally costs $60, what's the sale price?

**Reflection Questions:**
- Which category felt easiest? Why?
- Which category felt hardest? Why?
- Did any problem surprise you? (Either because you got it or because you couldn't figure it out)

**Submission:**
1. Complete all problems by hand on paper
2. For each problem, show your work step-by-step
3. Take clear photos of each page (make sure numbers are legible)
4. Paste images into a Google Doc
5. Add a short note: "Completed with / without difficulty," and "I'm most confident about ___, and least confident about ___"
6. Answer all reflection questions
7. Share the Google Doc with program staff

**Important:** You're not being evaluated on correctness. You're being evaluated on effort and honesty. If you don't know how to solve something, write "I don't know how to approach this" and move on. This tells us what to review.

### Task 2: "Ownership Reflection" (30 minutes)
**Objective:** Reflect on what ownership means to you and set intentions for how you'll practice it during the program.

**Instructions:**
Write a one-page reflection (250–400 words) in a Google Doc addressing the prompts below. These are for you and your program staff. Be honest. Vulnerability is not weakness; it's self-awareness. No formatting needed; clarity > perfection.

**A Time You Took Ownership**
- Describe a specific situation (school, work, personal project, relationship) where you took responsibility for something and saw it through.
- What did it feel like?
- What was the outcome?

**A Time You Avoided Ownership**
- Describe a specific situation where you didn't take responsibility (procrastinated, blamed someone else, gave up easily).
- What were you afraid of? What excuses did you make?
- What would have happened if you'd taken ownership?

**What You Want to Change**
- What's one area where you want to practice more ownership during this program?
- What does that look like concretely? (E.g., "When I don't understand something, I'll ask for help within 24 hours instead of staying silent.")
- How will you hold yourself accountable?

**Reflection Questions:**
- "When I re-read what I wrote, what emotion came up?"
- "Is there anything I want to change or add?"

### Part 4: Staff-Led Arithmetic Warm-Ups (35 minutes)
**Objective:** Review key arithmetic concepts and build confidence through guided practice.

- Order of Operations using PEMDAS (8 minutes)
<!-- Program Staff Demonstration:
- Write on screen: 10 + 2 × 3 = ?
  - "Without parentheses, which do we do first: addition or multiplication? (multiplication)
  - Step through: "First, 2 × 3 = 6. Then, 10 + 6 = 16."
  - Show common mistake: "If someone said 10 + 2 = 12, then 12 × 3 = 36, that would be wrong. We always do multiplication before addition."
  - Do 2–3 more examples, gradually increasing complexity
- Example: (8 - 3) × 2 + 4 ÷ 2 = ?
  - Parentheses first: 8 - 3 = 5
  - Then multiplication and division (left to right): 5 × 2 = 10; 4 ÷ 2 = 2
  - Then addition: 10 + 2 = 12

Interactive Element:
- Give participants one problem: "Solve this with me. Write down each step."
- Wait 2 minutes, then go through it together
- Ask: "Who got 12?" "Who got something different?" "What was different about your approach?"

Talking Point: "Order of operations is like grammar for math. Without it, everyone would interpret the same sentence differently. In code, we'll use this same principle with logic operators."
-->
- Working with Fractions (8 minutes)
<!-- Program Staff Demonstration:
- Start simple: 1/2 + 1/2 = 1 (same denominator, just add numerators)
- Build complexity: 1/2 + 1/3 = ?
  - "The denominators are different. We need a common denominator."
  - "What's a number both 2 and 3 go into? 6."
  - "1/2 = 3/6. 1/3 = 2/6. So 3/6 + 2/6 = 5/6."
- Show one multiplication example: 1/2 × 3/4 = 3/8 (multiply across)
- Emphasize: "You don't need to be fast at fractions. You just need to understand the concept: parts of a whole."

Interactive Element:
- Give one problem: "What's 2/3 + 1/6?"
- Have participants solve, then discuss

Talking Point: "Fractions show up everywhere: ratios, percentages, probabilities. Understanding fractions means understanding how to break complex problems into parts."
-->
- Percentages & Ratios (8 minutes)
<!-- Program Staff Demonstration:
- Percentage is "out of 100": 25% = 25/100 = 0.25
- "To find 25% of 80: 0.25 × 80 = 20"
- Ratio example: "If a class has a 2:3 ratio of boys to girls, and there are 10 boys, how many girls?" (10 ÷ 2 = 5 units; 5 × 3 = 15 girls)
- Real-world context: "A store is 30% off. A shirt costs $40. What's the sale price?"
- "30% off means you pay 70% of the original price."
- "0.70 × $40 = $28"

Interactive Element:
- Group problem: "A recipe serves 4 people and uses 2 cups of flour. You're cooking for 8 people. How much flour?"
- Participants discuss and solve (2–3 minutes)

Talking Point: "Percentages and ratios are how we think about scale: How does something change when we increase or decrease the inputs? That's what debugging is: 'If I change this value, what changes in the output?'"
-->
- Boolean Logic — True / False (8 minutes)

<!-- Program Staff Demonstration:

- Introduce the basics: True = 1, False = 0 (in most programming languages)
- AND: Both must be true
  - True AND True = True
  - True AND False = False
  - False AND False = False
- OR: At least one must be true
  - True OR False = True
  - False OR False = False
- NOT: Flips the value
  - NOT(True) = False
  - NOT(False) = True

Real-World Example:
- "You want to watch a movie. AND logic: You need money AND free time. If you have only one, you can't watch."
- "You want to go to the beach. OR logic: You can go on Saturday OR Sunday. You just need one day free."
- "NOT: You're NOT tired. (Opposite of tired = energized)"

Interactive Element:
- Scenario: "You're applying to a program. You need a 3.0 GPA AND completed prerequisites. Evaluate: Student A: 3.5 GPA, no prerequisites. Student B: 2.8 GPA, completed prerequisites. Student C: 3.2 GPA, completed prerequisites."
- A: False AND False = False (not admitted)
- B: False AND True = False (not admitted)
- C: True AND True = True (admitted)

Talking Point: "Every decision your code makes uses Boolean logic. If you want to display a button only for users who are logged in, that's a Boolean check. If you want to send an email to users who have notifications enabled AND haven't been emailed in a week, that's multiple Boolean checks combined."
-->

### Part 5: Mini-Challenge - Collaborative Logic Puzzles (20 minutes)
**Objective:** Apply arithmetic and logic thinking collaboratively. Experience how ownership shows up in a team. Work through together as a whole group or in smaller teams on 3-4 logic puzzles.

**Puzzle 1: The Logic Grid (5 minutes)**
*"Three students—Alice, Bob, and Carol—each have a favorite subject: Math, Science, or English. Each has a unique favorite. Use the clues to figure out who likes what."*

**Clues:**
- Alice doesn't like Math
- Bob likes either Math or Science
- The person who likes English also likes pizza (this is a quirky detail, but it helps narrow down)
- Carol doesn't like Science

<!-- Program Staff
Solution: Bob=Math, Alice=Science, Carol=English

Walk Through:
- "Carol doesn't like Science and there are only three subjects. If Alice doesn't like Math, Alice likes Science or English."
- "Bob likes Math or Science. If Alice likes Science, then Bob must like Math. That leaves Carol with English."
- "Does that fit all clues? Let's check. Yes!"

Talking Point: "You didn't need to know a formula. You needed to think logically, eliminate options, and keep track of constraints. That's exactly what debugging is."
-->

**Puzzle 2: The Number Puzzle (5 minutes)**
*"I'm thinking of a number. It's greater than 10 and less than 20. It's even. If you divide it by 3, the remainder is 1. What's the number?"*

<!-- Program Staff Notes:

Walk Through:
- "Even numbers between 10 and 20: 12, 14, 16, 18." 
- "Which have a remainder of 1 when divided by 3?"
- 12 ÷ 3 = 4 (remainder 0) — No
- 14 ÷ 3 = 4 remainder 2 — No
- 16 ÷ 3 = 5 remainder 1 — Yes!
- 18 ÷ 3 = 6 (remainder 0) — No
- "The answer is 16."

Interactive Element: Have participants solve the next puzzle on their own paper (2 minutes), then share answers

Talking Point: "You narrowed down possibilities by applying constraints. That's what Boolean logic and conditionals do in code."
-->

**Puzzle 3: Group Collaborative Puzzle (8 minutes)**
*"You have three containers: 5-liter, 3-liter, and a sink. Your goal is to measure exactly 4 liters. How do you do it?"*

<!-- Program Staff Notes:

Guiding Questions:

### Part 6: Ownership in Learning (5 minutes)

### Part 7: Q&A (5 minutes)
Q: "Will I need to memorize all these math formulas?"
A: No. We'll provide references, and you'll internalize what matters through practice.

Q: "I've always been bad at math. Can I really learn this?" 
A: Yes. The brain is plastic. Skills improve with practice and a growth mindset. You're already practicing.

Q: "What if I get stuck on something and can't figure it out?" 
A: Ask in #help, DM an program staff, come to office hours. Stuck is temporary; giving up is the only failure.

Q: "How does this relate to actual coding?" 
A: In code, logic is everything. Boolean operators, conditionals, loops—all built on Boolean logic. Arithmetic shows up in calculations, array indices, and performance optimization.
