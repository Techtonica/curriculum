### Motivation
Remote learning requires different skills than in-person learning. You'll spend your time during program (and similarly in your first techtonica role) on video calls, in Slack channels, collaborating asynchronously, using the internet, and being regularly exposed to new software. This session prepares you for that reality. Additionally, curiosity about how **_others_** use technology — not just exploring technology yourself — is how you learn diverse perspectives and empathy, both critical for working on teams. By interviewing someone about their digital habits, you're practicing the curiosity and communication skills that make remote teams successful.

### Objectives
By the end of this session, participants will:
- Set up professional digital tools (Slack and Zoom) correctly
- Understand digital etiquette and professionalism in remote settings
- Recognize how individuals differ in their technology comfort levels and use
- Practice empathy and curiosity by learning from others' technology experiences
- Understand that typing and writing are important skills for remote communication, but not perfection
- Build confidence navigating remote collaboration platforms
- Understand the difference between synchronous (live) and asynchronous (delayed) communication

### Specific Things to Learn

**Technical Skills:**
- Creating and customizing a professional Slack profile
- Sending and threading messages in Slack
- Joining channels and finding information in Slack
- Joining a Zoom meeting and testing audio/video
- Recognizing Zoom features: mute/unmute, camera on/off, screen share, chat, recording
- Bookmarking and organizing important links
- Loom video recording and sharing basics
- Understanding remote work platforms and tools

**Core "Soft" Skills:**
- Professional online presence and etiquette
- Asynchronous communication norms
- Camera presence and professionalism (lighting, background, behavior)
- Listening and curiosity when interviewing peers
- Articulating technical knowledge to non-technical audiences
- Understanding diverse technology backgrounds
- Empathy for varying comfort levels with digital tools

#### Task 1: "Digital Life Setup Challenge" (45–60 minutes)
**Objective:** Set up the core digital tools you'll use throughout the program and document the process.

**Instructions:**
Create a Google Doc to document each step with screenshots. For each item below, paste a screenshot showing completion:

**Part A: Slack Setup**
1. Create or access your Slack workspace profile (you'll be sent an invite link)
2. Upload a clear, professional photo as your profile picture (this can be casual/friendly, but should show your face clearly)
3. Add a bio: Write 2–3 sentences about yourself. Include: your name, your pronouns, and details that you would like others to know about you (i.e. where you're from, one fun fact, and what draws you to software engineering)
4. Send a scheduled message in your designated Slack channel introducing yourself (3–5 sentences), to release at a specific time.
5. Send an individual direct message to one peer in the program
6. Send a group direct message to your program staff
7. Set up settings to accommodate your program schedule and timezone, [learn more here](https://slack.com/help/articles/360025054173-Set-up-Slack-for-work-hours)
8. Reply in the thread of a peer's introduction post

**Checklist for screenshots:**
- Slack profile showing photo and bio
- Screenshot of your scheduled message that will be posted in Slack channel
- Screenshot of individual direct message sent to peer
- Screenshot of group direct message sent to program staff
- Screenshot of your thread response to a peer's introduction post

**Part B: Zoom Professionalism**
1. Set up a zoom account and adjust your settings so that your pronouns show next to your name when logged onto a video call
2. Test Zoom on your own and with a peer: Join a practice Zoom room with just yourself and with a peer.
3. Test your audio: Can you hear yourself? Does the microphone work?
4. Test your video: Can others see your face clearly? Is the lighting good?
5. Check your Zoom square for your name and pronouns
6. Adjust settings:
    - Turn on HD video (if available)
    - Check your background (tidy or use a virtual background if preferred)
    - Mute your microphone when not speaking (practice this)
7. Once you have confirmed all of your settings are adequately set up, type "Zoom set up complete" in the chat.
8. Take a screenshot showing yourself in the Zoom window with a clear video feed, microphone muted, your name and pronouns listed on the square, and your above chat message.
9. Take a screenshot showing yourself and a peer on a Zoom call with a clear video feed, a blurred background, microphone unmuted, your name and pronouns listed on the square, and your above chat message.

**Part C: Bookmarking & Organization**
1. Bookmark 3 program-related links in your browser. Examples:
    - Google calendar
    - Program curriculum
    - Freecodecamp
2. Create a folder in your bookmarks called "Program" and organize these 3 links there
3. Screenshot showing your organized bookmarks folder

**Tips for Success:**
- Give yourself adequate lighting when testing Zoom (sit near a window or lamp)
- For background, either use a tidy corner of your room or try a virtual background (Zoom > Settings > Background & Filters)
- Your bio doesn't need to be formal — be authentic! "I'm excited to learn, I love problem-solving, I'm from Texas" is perfect.
- If you're nervous about messaging peers, remember: everyone else is nervous too. Keep it friendly and ask a question.

#### Task 2: "Curiosity Interview" (30–40 minutes)
**Objective:** Practice curiosity and empathy by learning how others (of varying technical backgrounds) interact with technology.

**Instructions:**
1. **Find an Interview Subject:** Reach out to a friend, family member, or mentor who has a different relationship with technology than you do. This could be:
    - A parent or older relative
    - A friend who codes vs. someone who doesn't
    - Someone from a different career field
    - A peer in the program
2. **5–7 Interview Questions (examples below)**
    - What devices do you use most often (phone, laptop, tablet)?
    - Describe your typical day using technology. What do you use it for?
    - What's one technology skill you wish you had?
    - Tell me about a time technology frustrated you. What happened?
    - What's something you've learned about technology that surprised you?
    - How do you typically learn new tools or apps when you need to use them?
    - (Optional) What advice would you give to someone entering tech?

3. **Record a Video (5–10 minutes):**
    - Interview your subject over a video call and record the conversation with video recording setting such as those provided with [Loom](https://www.loom.com) or [Zoom](https://support.zoom.com/hc/en/article?id=zm_kb&sysparm_article=KB0076922)
    - Record yourself summarizing your interview in a 5–10 minute video. In the video, cover:
      - **Their background:** Brief intro to who they are and their tech comfort level
      - **Key discoveries:** 2–3 surprising or interesting things you learned
      - **What surprised you:** Something unexpected about their approach or perspective
      - **What you'll explore:** One thing they mentioned that made you curious, and how you'll investigate it
4. **Record & Share:**
    - Use Loom (free at [https://www.loom.com](https://www.loom.com)) to record a video walking through the Slack, Zoom, and other digital literacy skills you have learned
    - Get the shareable link, ensure its public, and submit it
3. Approximate length: 5–10 minutes

**Tips for Success:**
- Approach this with genuine curiosity, not judgment. There's no "right" way to use technology.
- If someone is less tech-savvy, that's perfect—you'll learn the most from perspectives different from yours.
- Listen for *patterns*: Do they learn by clicking around? By asking friends? By watching videos?
- Loom is intuitive—just hit record and talk. The goal is authenticity, not production quality.

**Free Resources:**
- **[Loom Setup Guide](https://www.loom.com/learn)** (includes tutorials on recording and sharing)
- **[YouTube - "Loom Tutorial"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JkPfQ7cLrMY)** (3 minutes)
- **Podcast: "How do people learn technology differently?"** (optional deeper dive)
- **[TED Talks — "The Power of Introverts" by Susan Cain](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=c0KYU2j0TM4)** (explores different learning styles)

**Program Staff Demo 1: Slack Professionalism & Etiquette (12 minutes)**
- **Profile & Presence:** Program staff will show their own Slack profile. Explain why a clear photo and bio matter (you're building trust with peers you might not meet in person)
- **Channel Etiquette:**
    - How to know which channel to post in (e.g., #general vs. #help vs. #random)
    - How to use threads to keep conversations organized (click "Reply in thread" rather than cluttering the channel)
    - When to use emoji reactions vs. writing a full message (reactions are quick, messages are thorough)
- **Direct Messages:** When to DM someone (quick question, private topic) vs. posting in a channel (so others can learn from the answer)
- **Asynchronous Communication:** Explain that in remote work, not everyone responds immediately. That's okay. You might ask a question in #tech-help at 9 AM and get answers at 3 PM—and that's fine.
- **Tone in Text:** Explain that tone is hard to read in text. When in doubt, use friendly emoji, positive words, and assume best intent from others.

**Example Scenario:**
"Let's say you have a coding question. Do you DM program staff or post in the Slack #tech-help channel?
- DM if: You think the question is specific to you, or you're embarrassed (no shame—we all are)
- Post in #help if: Other students might benefit from the answer (usually yes)
- Remember: Asking in public helps everyone learn. Most of your peers have the same questions."

**Program Staff Demo 2: Zoom Professionalism (12 minutes)**

- **Camera Etiquette:**
    - Camera on during class sessions (engagement & community)
    - Camera off is fine in large webinars or when you have technical issues
    - Position your camera at eye level (laptop screen or external monitor) so you're looking straight ahead
    - Check your background: tidy or virtual background—both are professional
- **Muting & Unmuting:**
    - Mute when not speaking (reduces background noise, respects others' audio)
    - Use the keyboard shortcut (usually Space to toggle mute temporarily)
    - Unmute before speaking so others can hear you
- **Screen Sharing:**
    - How to share your screen when presenting or asking for help
    - Making sure you're sharing the right window (not embarrassing personal files!)
- **Chat Function:**
    - Zoom chat is good for quick questions, but doesn't replace verbal communication
    - Save important info shared in chat—Zoom chats often disappear after the meeting
- **Recording:**
    - Understand that some sessions may be recorded for those who can't attend
    - Don't record without permission (it's usually stated when you join)

**Example Scenario:**
"You're in a Zoom class and you want to ask a question. What do you do?
- Raise your hand (click the icon) and wait to be called on, OR
- Ask in the chat (if the host encourages it), OR
- Wait for an open Q&A at the end
The point: be aware of what the facilitator prefers. Remote meetings work best when there's clear structure."

**Program Staff Demo 3: The Culture of Remote Learning & Typing/Writing (10 minutes)**

<!-- Program Staff Talking Point: You've interviewed people about technology. You probably noticed that they have different typing speeds, different comfort with writing, and different communication styles. That's going to be true of your cohort too. We celebrate diverse ways of communicating and working.
-->

- **Asynchronous vs. Synchronous:**
    - Synchronous (live): Zoom classes, real-time Slack conversations
    - Asynchronous (delayed): Recorded videos, email responses, forum posts
    - Both are legitimate. You might ask a question and wait for a response—that's normal.
- **Writing/Typing Skills:**
    - In remote work, writing clarity is critical. Your code comments, Slack messages, and email all communicate your ideas.
    - That said: **typos happen, grammar is secondary to clarity, and perfection isn't the goal.** We're a team of humans, not robots.
    - Spending 30 minutes to fix a typo in your Slack message? No. Sending a quick correction? Sure. Don't stress.
    - But do practice clear communication: "I'm stuck on this function because X, Y, Z. Here's what I tried. Can someone help?"
- **Typing Speed:**
    - Typing speed doesn't **_always_** determine your engineering ability. Some of the best engineers are slow typists (they're thinking deeply). Some are fast (they're used to rapid iteration). However, the program requires all participants to have capacity to type 50+ WPM with 80%+ accuracy
    - Fast typing helps with productivity, but it's not a prerequisite.
    - If you want to improve typing, resources exist (TypeRacer, TypingClub—both free), but it's not urgent.
    - Your fingers will speed up naturally as you code more.

**Example:**
"Here's a bad help question: 'code no work pls help'
Here's a good help question: 'My function is throwing 'Unexpected token' error on line 47. I'm trying to loop through an array, but I'm not sure if I have the syntax right. Here's my code [paste code]. What am I missing?'

The second one is clear, shows effort, and gives helpers context. It doesn't have perfect grammar—'I'm' could be 'I have', 'pls' should be 'please'—but that's fine. Clarity > perfection."

#### Part 6: Q&A (5 minutes)
- "How often should I check Slack?" → Depends on the channel. #announcements: multiple times daily. #random: when you have time. Set your notification preferences.
- "Is it unprofessional to have a pet/family member in the background during Zoom?" → Not at all. Remote work includes real life. If it's distracting, you can use a virtual background.
- "What if I'm not good at typing?" → You'll improve with practice. It's not a limiting factor for engineering.
- "Should I dress up for Zoom classes?" → Wear what makes you feel confident. A t-shirt is fine.
